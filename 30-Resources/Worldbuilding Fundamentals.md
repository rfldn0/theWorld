## Summary
Worldbuilding is the deliberate construction of a [[Secondary World]] governed by consistent internal rules. When those rules hold, they produce a [[Belief State]] — the reader's psychological acceptance of the world as real on its own terms. Two architectural approaches (top-down vs. bottom-up) and two systems philosophies (hard vs. soft) define the core design space. The [[Iceberg Theory]] governs how much of that world the writer should ever reveal.

## Main Content

### Top-Down vs. Bottom-Up Design
See: [[Top-Down Worldbuilding]], [[Bottom-Up Worldbuilding]]

**Top-down** begins at macro scale — continents, religions, millennia of history — before any localized scene is written. Structural consistency is strong, but the approach carries the risk of [[Worldbuilder's Disease]]: creative paralysis from over-engineering the world at the expense of writing the story.

**Bottom-up** starts at a single point of specificity — one town, one orphanage, one character — and expands outward only as the narrative demands it. Efficient and narratively relevant, but prone to logical inconsistencies and unexplained lore as scope widens.

Neither approach is superior. Most working writers hybridize: establish enough macro structure to avoid contradiction, then build locally.

### Hard vs. Soft Systems
See: [[Hard Magic Systems]], [[Soft Magic Systems]]

**Hard systems** define explicit rules, costs, and constraints on how power or law operates in the world. [[Brandon Sanderson]]'s "Laws of Magic" argue that a hard system becomes compelling precisely through its *limitations* — constraints force characters to solve problems creatively rather than overpower them. Example: the "law of equivalent exchange" in *Fullmetal Alchemist* is a hard system.

**Soft systems** leave their rules deliberately ambiguous, preserving mystery and inviting the audience to fill in the gaps. Studio Ghibli films and Tolkien's *The Lord of the Rings* are canonical examples — the magic feels real precisely because it is never fully explained.

Hard systems serve plot mechanics. Soft systems serve atmosphere and wonder.

### The Iceberg Theory
See: [[Iceberg Theory]]

A core discipline of worldbuilding: **only 10% of your constructed world should appear in the story**. The remaining 90% lives in notes and world bibles, shaping the texture of everything without being stated.

Attempting to surface the 90% produces info dumps — expository blocks that stall narrative momentum. The alternative is teaching world rules through *friction*: a character is fined for breaking a municipal law; a religious taboo is enforced through social shunning. The rule is demonstrated, never explained.

From the writer's perspective, this demands moral neutrality toward the world's own logic. A taboo act is punished because the world's structures enforce it — not because it is "bad." Good and bad characters are separated only by their actions in a given moment; the writer sees both as equal instruments of the story.

### Cognitive Estrangement and Ethical Worldbuilding
See: [[Cognitive Estrangement]]

Speculative fiction creates *defamiliarization*: placing recognizable human struggles — racism, structural oppression, environmental collapse — into an alien context strips away the reader's real-world assumptions, enabling fresh critical engagement with the issue.

This power carries an ethical obligation. Monolithic cultures ("all elves are good," "all dark races are evil") flatten the diversity and psychological depth worldbuilding is uniquely positioned to explore. Ethical worldbuilding means checking unconscious biases and ensuring the secondary world reflects genuine human complexity.

## Key Insights
- Top-down builds consistency; bottom-up builds relevance — both carry failure modes that discipline must prevent
- Hard systems make power interesting through constraint, not capability
- The Iceberg Theory is the primary tool against info-dumping: show friction, not exposition
- The writer's moral neutrality toward characters is a prerequisite for ethical worldbuilding, not a contradiction of it
- Speculative fiction's defamiliarization effect is a feature, not a side effect — it is the genre's primary mechanism for social critique
