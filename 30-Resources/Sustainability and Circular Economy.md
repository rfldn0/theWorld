## Summary
Sustainability asks whether current systems can persist without depleting the resources future generations need. The circular economy and biomimicry are design frameworks that move beyond damage reduction toward regenerative systems.

## Main Content

### Triple Bottom Line
Coined by [[John Elkington]]. Organizations are accountable across three dimensions:
- **People** — social equity, labor, community impact
- **Planet** — environmental footprint, resource use, emissions
- **Profit** — economic viability

Weakness: treated as a checklist rather than a genuine trade-off framework.

### Circular Economy
- **Linear model (old):** Take → Make → Dispose
- **Circular model:** Design waste out of the system entirely
- Resources stay in use as long as possible, then are recovered and regenerated
- Influenced by [[Cradle to Cradle]] (C2C) design by [[William McDonough]] and [[Michael Braungart]]

#### Cradle to Cradle Principles
1. **Waste = food** — all materials are nutrients for another cycle
2. **Biological cycle** — organic materials safely return to nature
3. **Technical cycle** — synthetic materials circulate at high quality indefinitely
4. **Renewable energy** — powered by current solar income, not stored carbon

### Biomimicry
- Learning from and emulating nature's 3.8 billion years of R&D
- Key thinker: [[Janine Benyus]]
- Nature as model, measure, and mentor
- **Examples:** Velcro (burr hooks), bullet train nose (kingfisher beak), self-cooling buildings (termite mounds)
- Core principle: nature runs on current energy, fits form to function, recycles everything

### Regenerative Organizations
Beyond "doing less harm" — actively restoring and regenerating systems
- Organizations that improve their environment by operating
- Linked to [[Biomimicry]] and living systems thinking

## Key Insights
- Sustainability is the minimum bar; the real goal is regeneration
- C2C reframes waste as a design failure, not an operational inevitability
- Biomimicry shifts the question from "how do we solve this?" to "how does nature solve this?"
- The circular economy requires systemic change — individual company efforts have limited impact without supply chain cooperation
