## Summary
Three dominant frameworks for evaluating moral decisions: utilitarianism judges by outcomes, deontology by duties and rules, and virtue ethics by character. Each captures something true but incomplete on its own.

## Main Content

### Utilitarianism
- **Core claim:** An action is right if it produces the greatest good for the greatest number
- **Key thinkers:** [[Jeremy Bentham]], [[John Stuart Mill]]
- **Strength:** Practical, measurable, considers everyone affected
- **Weakness:** Can justify harming a minority for majority benefit; hard to quantify "good"
- **Business application:** Cost-benefit analysis, stakeholder impact assessments

### Deontology
- **Core claim:** Some actions are inherently right or wrong regardless of consequences
- **Key thinker:** [[Immanuel Kant]] — Categorical Imperative
  - *Act only according to rules you could will to be universal law*
  - *Treat people as ends in themselves, never merely as means*
- **Strength:** Protects individual rights; consistent principles
- **Weakness:** Can produce bad outcomes when rules conflict; rigid
- **Business application:** Corporate codes of conduct, fiduciary duties, informed consent

### Virtue Ethics
- **Core claim:** Focus on the character of the agent, not the act or its consequences
- **Key thinker:** [[Aristotle]] — eudaimonia (human flourishing)
- **Central question:** "What would a person of good character do?"
- **Key virtues:** Honesty, courage, justice, prudence, integrity
- **Strength:** Addresses moral motivation, not just behavior
- **Weakness:** Vague guidance in specific dilemmas; culturally variable
- **Business application:** Leadership character, organizational culture, hiring for values

### Comparison

| | Utilitarianism | Deontology | Virtue Ethics |
|-|---------------|------------|---------------|
| Focus | Outcomes | Rules/duties | Character |
| Question | What produces most good? | What is my duty? | Who am I becoming? |
| Weakness | Minority harm | Rigid rules | Vague guidance |

## Key Insights
- No single framework is sufficient — real ethical reasoning uses all three as lenses
- Deontology sets the floor (what you must never do); utilitarianism guides optimization above that floor; virtue ethics shapes who decides
- [[Business Ethics]] adds stakeholder complexity: shareholders vs. employees vs. society vs. environment
