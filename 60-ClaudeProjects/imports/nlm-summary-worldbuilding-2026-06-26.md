## Summary
Worldbuilding is the meticulous construction of a Secondary World governed by consistent internal rules that generate reader belief. The source covers two design approaches (top-down vs. bottom-up), two system philosophies (hard vs. soft), the Iceberg Technique for managing lore reveal, and the ethical responsibility that comes with speculative fiction's defamiliarization power.

## Ollama Output — Summarize

### Core Thesis
Worldbuilding involves constructing a Secondary World governed by internal rules, which creates reader immersion through consistent adherence. The most effective speculative worldbuilding balances detailed rule systems with narrative relevance, using the iceberg principle and cognitive estrangement for deeper engagement.

### Key Points
- **Approach:** Two main methods — Top-Down (starting macro-scale, high risk of paralysis) or Bottom-Up (starting small/narrative-driven)
- **System Rigor:** Systems can be Hard (clear rules, limitations crucial) or Soft (ambiguous rules, encourages audience imagination)
- **Iceberg Principle:** Only 10% of world lore should appear in the story; the rest must remain hidden
- **Avoiding Info Dumps:** Lore demonstrated through "friction" — characters facing consequences within their society or magic system
- **Ethical Dimension:** Speculative fiction's power for cognitive estrangement necessitates ethical worldbuilding to avoid simplistic stereotypes

### Conclusions
Successful speculative worldbuilding requires meticulous construction of internal rules while maintaining narrative focus. Consistency over exhaustive detail on-page ("iceberg technique"), teaching through consequences rather than exposition, and ethical responsibility to avoid reductive stereotypes.
